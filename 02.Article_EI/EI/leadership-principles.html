Foundation

The Trust Equation

Trust is not a feeling — it is engineered, observable, and measurable. The Trust Equation breaks trustworthiness into four levers a leader can deliberately move: Credibility plus Reliability plus Intimacy, divided by Self-Orientation.

C

Credibility

The words we speak. Built through expertise, evidence, candour, and the discipline of saying "I don't know" when it's true.

R

Reliability

The actions we take. Small, kept commitments compound. Reliability is the operating system of trust.

I

Intimacy

The safety to be human. People entrust you with what matters when they believe you'll handle it with care.

S

Self-Orientation

The denominator. Lower is better. The more focused on your own agenda, the less trust you generate.

Most trust failures I have seen in 20+ years of leading data and risk teams are not failures of credibility. The numerator is usually fine — leaders are clever, deliver, and care. The damage almost always sits in the denominator: self-orientation in the room. A meeting where the senior leader needs to be the smartest, a 1:1 where the agenda is really a status update for the boss, a feedback session designed to discharge the leader's frustration. Credibility, Reliability and Intimacy can all be high and trust will still collapse because the denominator is louder than everything above the line.

Trust is built in millimetres and broken in metres. The denominator is what most leaders forget to watch.
Foundations

Emotional Intelligence — the four moves and the five components

Daniel Goleman's What Makes a Leader? (HBR, 1998) named the five components that distinguish effective leaders from merely clever ones. They translate cleanly into four observable, daily moves at work.

Goleman's five components

Self-awareness — knowing your emotions, strengths, limits and impact.
Self-regulation — the deliberate pause; choosing response over reaction.
Motivation — drive that goes beyond status or money toward meaningful goals.
Empathy — understanding others' emotions and treating their context as data.
Social skill — friendliness with a purpose; moving people in a useful direction.

The four observable moves

1. Read the room — including yourself. Notice what's actually being felt before you decide what's being said.
2. Choose your response. Insert the gap. Deliberate is not impassive.
3. Influence the emotional weather. The senior person sets the climate, whether they intend to or not.
4. Care on purpose. Empathy as a practice, not a personality trait.

1. Reading the Room — Including Yourself +

The most under-practised EI skill is noticing your own state before acting on it. Most poor decisions in meetings, hiring panels and escalations skip this step entirely. Before you respond, scan three layers: what am I feeling, what is the room feeling, and what am I about to do because of it?

Reading the room well requires curiosity rather than diagnosis. Ask "what's going on for them?" before "what's wrong with them?" Curiosity is the discipline that lets you act on the situation in front of you, not the script in your head.

2. Choosing Your Response +

Self-regulation is the deliberate pause between stimulus and response. Leaders who never react are not impassive — they are simply unwilling to outsource their behaviour to whoever happens to be provoking them.

A practical test: in your last difficult conversation, did you respond to the person in front of you, or to the version of them already living in your head? If the latter, the pause is the work.

3. Influencing the Emotional Weather +

The most senior person in any room sets the weather, whether they want to or not. A sharp aside, a checked phone, a tone of dismissal — these become the climate. Equally, a deliberate exhale, a thank-you, a slow question, a moment of honesty — these become the climate too.

Influencing weather is not theatre. It is the conscious choice to lift energy when it sags, to cool tempers without shaming, and to create the conditions where good thinking is even possible.

4. Caring on Purpose +

Caring on purpose means taking people's context, constraints and intent seriously — even when you disagree with their conclusion. It is what turns clever leaders into trusted ones.

The opposite of caring on purpose is not cruelty. It is convenience: caring when it costs nothing and disappearing when it does.

Framework

The EQ-i 2.0 — fifteen subscales of emotional intelligence

The EQ-i 2.0® (based on the Bar-On model) is the most widely used measure of EI. It maps fifteen subscales across five composites — and unlike many leadership models, it treats emotional intelligence as a developable, not fixed, capability.

I use the EQ-i 2.0 because it is dynamic and circular. There is no start or end point: self-perception fuels self-expression, which shapes interpersonal life, which improves decision-making, which enables stress management, which deepens self-perception again. Each composite relies on and influences the others, and none is more important than the rest.

CompositeSubscalesWhat it asks of a leader
Self-Perception Self-Regard · Self-Actualization · Emotional Self-Awareness Do you know who you are, what excites you, and how your emotions are landing on others?
Self-Expression Emotional Expression · Independence · Assertiveness Can you say what you feel and think — constructively, without waiting for permission?
Interpersonal Interpersonal Relationships · Empathy · Social Responsibility Do people feel taken seriously by you, or merely processed by you?
Decision Making Problem Solving · Reality Testing · Impulse Control Can you make sound calls when the room — and you — are emotionally loaded?
Stress Management Flexibility · Stress Tolerance · Optimism Can you cope with change, hold hope, and adapt without losing your edge?

Five subscales worth deepening

Self-Regard — appreciating yourself, "warts and all" +

Self-Regard is the foundation. It is the ability to respect and accept yourself — knowing your strengths and limitations and liking yourself anyway. Leaders with healthy Self-Regard openly acknowledge mistakes and can say "I don't know" without flinching.

Signs to develop more Self-Regard: persistent self-doubt despite evidence of competence, harshly critical self-assessment, difficulty accepting compliments, over-concern with others' opinions, avoidance of challenges out of fear of failure.

How to build it: practise self-compassion, replace negative self-talk with realistic affirmations, set and celebrate small achievements, build self-awareness through reflection or journaling, and establish healthy boundaries.

Story · Angela, mid-40s executive

Decisive, respected, juggling ministers and stakeholders. Behind closed doors, a relentless inner critic and quiet imposter feelings. Her EQ-i 2.0 profile showed high Self-Actualization but lagging Self-Regard — the perfect explanation for the perfectionism, stress and "never enough" feeling. She did not silence the critic; she got curious about it. The critic still shows up, but no longer runs the show.

Self-Actualization — pursuing what actually matters +

Self-Actualization is the willingness to keep improving and to engage in personally meaningful work. It is one of the EQ-i subscales most strongly tied to leadership capability — particularly Innovation, Insight, Authenticity and Coaching.

Signs to develop more Self-Actualization: chronic lack of fulfilment even when goals are hit, restlessness or boredom, frequent envy, neglected interests and passions, difficulty adapting to change.

How to build it: set personal goals tied to values, seek mentoring, deliberately seek new experiences, embrace lifelong learning.

Story · Mike, senior banker

Three decades up the ranks, then quietly cruising toward retirement. His low Self-Actualization signalled to his team that mediocrity was acceptable. One coaching question — "What do you want your legacy to be?" — re-ignited him. He chose to spend his last chapter lifting others, not coasting.

Emotional Expression — saying the "why" behind the what +

Emotional Expression is the outward communication of feelings — through words, tone, body language. Leaders who can express emotions appropriately create authentic connection and earn buy-in that pure logic cannot.

Build it by: labelling your emotions out loud ("I'm frustrated that…", "I'm encouraged by…"), expanding your emotional vocabulary beyond mad/sad/glad, and checking your read on others ("It looks like you're sceptical — am I reading that right?").

Story · Mannat, executive director

Brilliant analyst, clean strategy, disengaged stakeholders. Her low Emotional Expression score explained it: the data was right but the human "why" was missing. She didn't change her message — she changed how she opened it. One slide on how people felt now and how they could feel after the change. The room leaned in.

Independence — standing on your own two feet +

Independence is the ability to be self-directed and free from emotional dependency on others — without becoming isolated. Independent leaders consult, but they do not outsource the decision. They can stand by an unpopular call.

The shadow of high Independence is forgetting that your team is not you. What feels obvious to you may feel invisible to them.

Story · Melissa, fast-paced food services lead

Naturally independent and high-performing — newly promoted, hitting targets. Engagement scores landed below average. Her team said they only heard from her when something went wrong. She didn't need affirmation, so she had assumed her team didn't either. They did. She built simple, deliberate feedback loops; engagement and trust climbed.

Flexibility — movement as progress, not disruption +

Flexibility is the ability to adapt to unfamiliar, unpredictable and fluid circumstances — to change your mind when evidence suggests you should. Far from weakness, it takes courage. "I thought I was right, and I'm a big enough person to adjust."

Build it by treating challenges as opportunities to learn, seeking diverse perspectives, running "what if" scenarios, anchoring on long-term vision with short-term agility, and surrounding yourself with a trusted advisory board.

Story · Naomi, senior policy advisor

Consistency was her strength; reform exposed it as a constraint. Her low Flexibility wasn't a habit — it was driven by a need for competence and a fear of letting people down. With practice, she learned to ask "is this a crisis, or just a shift?" before reacting. She still prefers predictability, but now sees movement as part of progress, not just disruption.

Source: Emotional Intelligence for Leaders, Coaches and Changemakers — Rosalinda Batson & Dr John Gora, Neural Networks Concepts (2026), based on the EQ-i 2.0® / Bar-On model owned by MHS Assessments.

Personality

Hogan — the bright side, dark side, and inside

Hogan assessments are widely used in executive selection and development. They map three views of personality that no leader can afford to be naive about: how you behave at your best, what derails you under pressure, and what actually drives you.

Bright side · HPI

The Hogan Personality Inventory describes day-to-day, "at your best" personality. It predicts strengths, work behaviour, and leadership style — the version of you others usually meet.

Dark side · HDS

The Hogan Development Survey identifies eleven derailers — overused strengths that emerge under stress and quietly damage trust, relationships and careers.

Inside · MVPI

The Motives, Values, Preferences Inventory describes what gets you out of bed — the goals, values, drives and interests that determine whether a culture will fit you and you will fit a culture.

The eleven Hogan derailers

Derailers are not weaknesses; they are strengths used too much, too often, or in the wrong context. Self-aware leaders learn to spot them in themselves — especially under pressure, when blind spots get loud.

Presence

How leaders show up — presence and curiosity

Long before anyone hears the strategy, they read the leader. Presence is the unspoken information your team gathers from how you walk in, who you greet, what you notice, and what you ask.

HBR's research on executive presence converges on three components: gravitas (the substance and steadiness behind your words), communication (the craft of being understood), and appearance (the signals your physical presence sends). What changes by context is the weight of each — but the test never changes: do people leave the room thinking better, or feeling smaller?

Curiosity is the engine. Great leaders demonstrate consistent curiosity about four things: the values and motivations of their people, the needs of customers, their own changing role, and the shifting context of the business itself. When leaders are visibly present and visibly curious, others feel seen, heard and acknowledged — the precondition for trust and the foundation of real influence.

Presence is a choice, not a personality

Quiet leaders can have profound presence. Loud leaders can have none. Presence is the deliberate decision to show up engaged, calm, and interested in the people in front of you — whether the meeting is critical or routine.

Curiosity replaces certainty

Leaders who substitute "I think you're missing X" with "What am I missing?" change the room. Curiosity is humility in working clothes — it produces better answers and better relationships at the same time.

Three questions a curious leader asks before deciding +
  • What do I not yet understand about this? — exposes assumptions before they cost you.
  • Who has the most to lose from this decision, and have I heard them? — separates the loud voices from the relevant ones.
  • What would change my mind? — protects you from confirmation bias dressed up as conviction.
Signals leaders send without realising +
  • Who you make eye contact with first.
  • Whose ideas you build on (and whose you simply repeat back as your own).
  • What you laugh at, and at whose expense.
  • How long you let silence sit before filling it.
  • What you do with your phone in the room.

Sources: HBR — The New Rules of Executive Presence; HBR — Leaders, Make Curiosity the Core of Your Organizational Culture.

Candour

Silence is not agreement

Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety is unambiguous: people default to silence. Polite workplaces without candour silence their best thinking. The leader's job is not to encourage talk — it is to make the right kind of voice and the right kind of silence both possible.

Psychological safety is the shared belief that you will not be punished or humiliated for admitting a mistake, raising a concern, or disagreeing with the most senior person in the room. It is not niceness. It is the working condition under which candour becomes routine. Edmondson's voice-and-silence framework distinguishes productive contributing and productive processing from unproductive withholding and unproductive disrupting. Both speech and silence can serve a team — or quietly poison it.

A nodding room is not a convinced room. The most expensive failures I have seen in data and risk happened in meetings where everyone "agreed" and no one believed it. If you are the senior voice, your default assumption should be that you are not hearing the full picture. Make it safe — and routine — to be told.

Silence vs agreement — a working table

BehaviourWhat it can look likeWhat it usually meansLeader move
Productive voice Disagrees clearly with reasons; offers alternatives. Trust is high enough to spend. Thank visibly. Build on it. Reinforce that this is the norm.
Productive silence Pause to think; lets others speak first; reflects before responding. Considered processing; respect for others' airtime. Protect it. Don't fill the gap. Invite written input later.
Withholding Polite nods, "sounds good", no questions, no objections. Disagreement that did not feel safe to voice. Surface it: "What's the strongest argument against this?"
Disrupting Speaking over others, scoring points, performative dissent. Voice without psychological safety for everyone else. Name the pattern in private. Reset the meeting norms.
Compliance theatre Visible agreement in the room, real disagreement in the corridor. The decision was already made; the meeting was performance. Stop running decisions as meetings. Decide; then consult on execution.
"Don't rock the boat" and "no one ever got fired for silence" are the most expensive sentences in any organisation. — after Amy Edmondson, HBR
Five moves that turn silence into signal +
  • Frame the work as learning, not performance. "We are figuring this out" lowers the cost of being wrong.
  • Speak last. Senior voices first compress everyone else's. Hold your view until you have heard others'.
  • Ask for the dissent you most expect. "Who thinks this won't work, and why?" is more useful than "Any questions?".
  • Reward the messenger. The first person who challenges you publicly determines whether the next twenty will.
  • Distinguish quiet from quiet. Some silence is thinking. Some silence is fear. Learn the difference, person by person.

Sources: HBR — What People Get Wrong About Psychological Safety; HBR — Creating Psychological Safety in the Workplace (Edmondson).

Inclusion

Engaging introverts and silent doers

Most meetings are designed for extroverts and rewarded by talkers. The leader who only hears the loudest voices is, by definition, missing some of the best thinking in the room. Introvert engagement is not a soft skill — it is an information strategy.

Introverts are not less interested, less committed, or less capable. They typically need time to think before speaking, format options for contributing, and safety from being put on the spot. When you give them all three, the best ideas in the room often come from the people who said the least in the first ten minutes.

Designing meetings that work for both

DimensionDefault extrovert designInclusive design that works for both
Generating ideas Open brainstorm; loudest voices set the frame. Brainwriting — silent individual writing first, then share. Round-robin to surface every voice.
Pre-reads Decks shown live; reaction expected in real time. Pre-reads sent 24 hours ahead. Comments invited in writing before the meeting.
Asking for input "Any thoughts?" — answered by whoever speaks fastest. "Take a minute, write a sentence, then we'll go round." Or pair-share, then report back.
Calling on people Cold-calling introverts on the spot. Forewarn: "I'd love your view in five minutes — anything you want to flag now?"
After the meeting Decisions stick from whoever spoke last. Open async window — channel, doc, or 1:1 — to capture late, considered input.
Seven concrete techniques you can use this week +
  • Brainwrite before brainstorming. Two minutes of silent writing before any open discussion.
  • Round-robin with the option to pass. Everyone gets the floor; no one is forced to fill it.
  • Sub-groups before plenary. Trying out an idea in a group of three is far easier than trying it out in a group of fifteen.
  • Pre-reads, not surprises. Send the question ahead. The best thinking rarely happens at the speed of conversation.
  • Written backchannels. A meeting doc, a Slack thread, an anonymous form — give a written exit for ideas that didn't get airtime.
  • The "follow-up 24" rule. Decisions taken in the room are revisited 24 hours later before going final, so reflectors can weigh in.
  • 1:1 cadence. Most introverts contribute their best thinking 1:1, not in groups. A regular 30-minute slot will outperform any town hall.
What not to do +
  • Don't cold-call as a "test". You'll get an answer, not the best answer — and they'll go quieter for the rest of the meeting.
  • Don't equate quiet with disengaged. The person who hasn't spoken often has the most considered position in the room.
  • Don't ask introverts to "be more visible" without redesigning the meeting. Visibility is a shared design problem, not a personal flaw.
  • Don't reward the first hand up. Reward the best argument, regardless of when or how it arrived.
Belonging

Encouraging individuality — and the power of mattering

The opposite of inclusion is not exclusion; it is sameness. Teams that produce truly novel work are teams in which people are encouraged to be themselves on purpose — and made to feel that their distinct contribution is seen.

HBR's research on mattering at work makes a sharp claim: many employees do not feel that they matter to their employers, bosses or colleagues. Mattering is the experience of feeling significant — knowing you are valued and that you add value. It is built in micro-moments of being seen and heard, and scaled by leaders who turn it into a cultural norm. Where mattering is high, engagement, retention, productivity and innovation all move with it.

Strengths-based leadership is the operational route to mattering. When development focuses on amplifying people's actual strengths — rather than endlessly remediating weaknesses — the results show up in measurable terms: engagement, retention, performance, innovation. Individuality is not a tolerance to manage; it is a competitive advantage to cultivate.

See people, then deploy them

Spend more time understanding the unique strengths of each person on your team than you spend designing job descriptions. Then shape the work around the talent, not the other way round.

Affirm significance, often

Mattering is built in repeated micro-moments: naming a contribution, quoting someone in their absence, crediting an idea publicly, asking a follow-up question that proves you actually listened.

Make it cultural, not personal

If only one leader does this, it is a personality. If the leadership team does it consistently, it becomes the operating culture — and the retention case writes itself.

A short audit — does individuality survive on your team? +
  • Can each person on your team articulate their top three strengths — and do you agree with their list?
  • Have you redesigned anyone's role in the last six months to better match their strengths?
  • Whose distinct voice would your team most miss if they left tomorrow — and have you told them so?
  • When was the last time you defended an unconventional approach from a team member to your own boss?
  • Do new joiners stay themselves over time, or quietly converge to look like the rest of the team?

Sources: HBR — The Power of Mattering at Work; Zenger Folkman — Strengths-Based Leadership Development.

Application

Principles in action

A leadership framework only matters if it survives contact with a Tuesday afternoon. These are the principles I try to live by — derived from the Trust Equation, EQ-i 2.0, Hogan, and HBR — translated into practice.

Feedback from care, not from frustration

360-degree feedback is most useful when it is anchored in care for the person and clarity about the work. Focus on prioritisation, on protecting their responsibilities and bandwidth — not just on cataloguing failures. Feedback delivered to discharge your frustration has a half-life of about an hour and a trust cost that lasts months.

People as priority, process as servant

The role is demanding for everyone. Evaluation should improve the process and safeguard the contribution. The question is rarely "is this person good enough?" — it is "is the system around them set up for them to be good?"

Lower the denominator before raising the numerator

Most trust gains come not from being more credible, more reliable or more intimate — but from visibly reducing self-orientation. Watch for who you make the meeting about.

Choose your weather, then your words

The room reads your state before it reads your slides. The exhale, the tone, the pace of your first question — these set the conditions everything else operates in.

Treat silence as data

If the room is quiet, it is telling you something. Find out whether it is thought, fear, or boredom — and respond to the actual signal.

Make individuality the rule, not the exception

If you can name what is uniquely valuable about each person on your team — and they would agree with your list — you have a team. If you can't, you have a head-count.

Leadership is not what you do in the moment of decision. It is the climate you have built so that, by the time the moment arrives, the right decision is already half-made.
